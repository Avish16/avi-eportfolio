Looking back at the M.S. in Applied Data Science

Long-form reflection · Avi Sharma · Syracuse University

This reflection focuses on how my M.S. journey shaped my approach to applied data science: building reliable pipelines, evaluating models with discipline, and communicating results for real-world decisions.

What I thought graduate school would be

Before I started the M.S. in Applied Data Science, I expected a heavy technical curriculum focused mostly on algorithms, model theory, and coding assignments. I assumed most projects would look like clean benchmark tasks with fixed labels and clear leaderboards.

That expectation was only partially correct. The program is technically rigorous, but the larger shift came from learning to handle realistic constraints at the same time: messy data, imperfect labels, stakeholder context, reproducibility requirements, and responsible model use. I learned that high performance matters, but dependable workflows and clear interpretation matter just as much.

I also underestimated how much time we would spend on the “messy middle”: joins that do not quite match, documentation that matters for teams, and the translation layer between a business question (“Are we targeting the right neighborhoods?”) and a modeling question (“What label should we predict, and at what geography?”). That messy middle is where applied data science actually lives.

Another expectation I had to revise was the idea that there is one correct toolchain. In practice, I moved across Python notebooks, SQL warehouses, BI dashboards, cloud services, and web applications. That felt fragmented initially, but it reflects real data science work where value comes from connecting analysis, engineering, and communication in one workflow.

Finally, I came in expecting a clear boundary between “research” and “application.” The coursework blurred that boundary in a productive way. Human-centered AI, for example, is not purely academic; it changes how you prompt, evaluate, and structure LLM systems. Deep learning is not only about architectures; it is about data augmentation and error analysis when labels are scarce. Those lessons do not stay in the classroom - they show up in how I scope my GitHub projects and how I talk about limitations in interviews.

What I learned, tied to program outcomes

Every program frames outcomes differently; in my own words, I wanted to graduate able to (1) build reliable models in realistic settings, (2) ship complete workflows - not orphan notebooks, (3) explain trade-offs to non-experts, and (4) apply responsible practices when data involves people, policy, or safety-critical contexts. The following sections connect those goals to what I did and how I changed.

Technical foundations that stuck

The coursework across applied machine learning, deep learning, natural language processing, and quantitative analysis gave me a coherent map of when to use which family of tools. I learned to stop treating “the best model” as a universal concept and to start with constraints: data volume, label quality, latency needs, interpretability requirements, and maintenance cost. Transfer learning is not just a buzzword when you work with medical imaging or limited labels; it is a practical strategy. Gradient boosting remains a workhorse for structured data. Time series methods matter when seasonality and drift dominate. And generative models and large language models are not magic - they are systems that need retrieval, evaluation, and guardrails when factual grounding matters.

Perhaps the most important habit I built is disciplined evaluation: separating training optimism from generalization, using validation thoughtfully, and documenting failure modes. A number on a test set is not the end of the story; it is a starting point for asking what would break the model in deployment.

Natural language processing was especially transformative because the field shifted during my degree. I studied classical embeddings and sequence models, then watched transformer-based approaches become the default, and then watched retrieval-augmented generation become a practical necessity for factual grounding. That progression taught me that fundamentals matter - tokenization, evaluation metrics, and understanding bias - but so does adaptability. The syllabus is a moving target; the skill is knowing how to read a paper, reproduce a minimal experiment, and decide whether a technique is worth integrating into a larger system.

Business analytics and advanced database coursework reinforced something I used to rush past: the definition of a metric. Revenue per customer sounds simple until you ask which time window, which currency, which handling of returns, and which cohort definition. I began to treat metric definitions as part of the model specification, not as a footnote. That sounds bureaucratic, but it prevents the subtle error where a model optimizes perfectly for a metric that does not match the decision the business actually needs.

End-to-end thinking

Graduate training pushed me beyond “fit a model in a notebook.” In my internship and in projects like MediExplain and the drowsiness system, success meant connecting preprocessing, modeling, monitoring hooks (even simple ones), and a user-facing artifact - an app, a visualization, or a documented pipeline. I began to see notebooks as a development environment, not the final deliverable, unless the deliverable is explicitly a research report.

This mindset also changed how I approach collaboration. When retrieval databases, validation layers, and UI flows enter the picture, interfaces between components become first-class concerns. That is a different engineering skill than tuning hyperparameters, and it is increasingly central in applied AI work.

End-to-end thinking also includes data hygiene at scale. In my internship, processing terabytes of sensing data made clear that the “interesting” ML work is often downstream of unglamorous but essential steps: deduplication, alignment across sensors, handling missingness, and versioning of feature definitions. I learned to respect those steps as part of the scientific contribution, not as a hurdle to skip. The same principle applies to smaller projects: if I cannot reproduce a feature table from raw inputs, I do not fully understand my pipeline.

Communication and audience

I used to think communication meant simplifying results. Now I think it means choosing fidelity appropriately: not dumbing down, but sequencing ideas so a city stakeholder can understand why a map matters, or a clinician-adjacent partner can understand what an LLM system can and cannot promise. Visualizations are not decoration; they are arguments. Writing for this ePortfolio - especially the overview and project pages - was practice in keeping technical accuracy while remaining accessible.

Another communication lesson is managing uncertainty explicitly. Non-experts are not afraid of uncertainty; they are afraid of hidden uncertainty. I learned to pair point estimates with plain-language caveats: what data the model never saw, what drift could mean, and what would trigger a retrain. That habit came from coursework, from research presentations, and from building tools that real people might click through.

Responsible and human-centered AI

Courses and research in human-centered AI reframed how I design systems. For healthcare-adjacent applications, “accuracy” without transparency can harm trust. That is why MediExplain emphasizes structured outputs, validation, and retrieval grounding rather than a single chatbot that free-associates. In safety-sensitive computer vision, false alarms erode trust quickly; that is why the drowsiness project includes temporal aggregation and threshold tuning - not just a frame-level classifier score.

Privacy, consent, and clarity about limitations are not separate topics; they shape architecture. That lesson will stay with me regardless of whether I work in healthcare, finance, or public-sector analytics.

I also learned to treat evaluation as a social process, not only a technical one. When stakeholders ask “Is it safe?” they are often asking whether the failure modes are understood and monitored. Building JSON validation and retry logic in MediExplain is not glamorous, but it is a concrete way to reduce unpredictable outputs. Similarly, when working with geospatial data in public settings, it matters how results could be interpreted by policymakers or community members; I try to pair technical work with narrative about what the map does not say.

Three projects that shaped my education

Among the work represented on my GitHub profile, three projects capture different facets of my graduate journey: MediExplain, driver drowsiness detection, and the Syracuse DataThon spatial analytics effort. Each one taught me something the others could not.

MediExplain: building an LLM system, not a demo

MediExplain sits at the intersection of NLP coursework, human-centered design, and the realities of medical information asymmetry. The project forced me to think in components: routing user intent, retrieving evidence, generating explanations, validating structure, and recovering from errors. It also forced me to confront what “helpful” means - patients and caregivers need different layers of detail, and the system must remain understandable rather than merely fluent.

Technically, implementing RAG with ChromaDB and a curated corpus showed me where retrieval quality dominates model quality. If retrieval fails, even a strong LLM will sound confident about the wrong context. Building guardrails - JSON validation, retries, consistency checks - was tedious at times, but it mirrored what production teams do when outputs must be inspectable. Streamlit gave me a rapid path to a usable interface, which matters because the best internal model is useless if nobody can interact with it safely.

Driver drowsiness detection: from images to decisions over time

The drowsiness project was a lesson in classical deep learning craftsmanship: data augmentation, architecture choices, and rigorous metrics like ROC-AUC in addition to accuracy. But the more interesting lesson was temporal reasoning. Eyes close naturally many times; sustained closure is different. Building a pipeline that extracts eye regions, fuses left and right predictions, and aggregates signals over time mirrored how real perception systems are engineered - multiple stages, each with failure modes.

OpenCV deployment paths for webcam and offline video reminded me that compute budgets and frame rates matter. Optimizations like frame skipping are not “cheating”; they are part of making a system usable. That connects directly to lessons from my coursework in deep learning about inference efficiency and to my broader belief that applied ML must include deployment thinking early, not as an afterthought.

DataThon: geospatial storytelling for civic data

The DataThon project anchored me in messy civic datasets: violations, assessments, and spatial joins that do not line up perfectly with textbook examples. Working with teammates to build a grid over Syracuse and produce Folium maps taught me how much communication depends on cartography - color scales, legends, and interactivity shape whether a model’s output becomes actionable for someone who does not read Python.

This project also reinforced ethics of interpretation. Maps can suggest certainty; we had to be careful about what the model implied versus what the data supported. That tension - between a compelling visualization and an honest limitation statement - is a recurring theme in public-facing analytics.

Collaboration on the datathon also sharpened my project management instincts: splitting tasks, aligning on schema decisions early, and keeping a shared narrative for judges and the public. The repository is not only code - it is evidence of how a team turned a fuzzy civic question into a reproducible pipeline and a set of interactive artifacts.

Internship and research outside the classroom

My data science internship at MoMacMo exposed me to scale I could not replicate in homework alone: terabyte-scale distributed acoustic sensing data processed on AWS, exploratory analysis across spatiotemporal dimensions, and models for detecting and classifying seismic events. The work demanded both signal-processing intuition and modern ML (including transfer learning with architectures like VGG16 alongside tree-based models such as XGBoost). It reinforced that “big data” is not just storage - it is orchestration, monitoring, and careful feature design when labels are imperfect.

As an AI researcher in Healthcare AI at Syracuse, I focused on medical image classification with CNNs and transfer learning, plus rigorous preprocessing and augmentation. Presenting to stakeholders and contributing to a project recognized as Best Project in Fall 2025 sharpened my ability to connect experimental results to narrative: what changed, why it matters, and what we would try next.

Together, internship and research rounded out coursework. Class taught methods; these roles taught constraints - timelines, data access, and the need for clarity when experts depend on your outputs.

My favorite class - and why

If I must choose a single favorite, I gravitate toward a course that combined depth with relevance - often deep learning or natural language processing, depending on the semester - because those fields evolve quickly and reward both mathematical intuition and engineering experimentation. The “favorite” is less about the textbook and more about the moment a concept clicks: when you see why attention mechanisms matter, or why naive fine-tuning fails on small medical datasets. The classes that pushed me to implement ideas end-to-end, not just pass a quiz, are the ones I still think about when I start a new project.

Human-centered AI also stands out because it changed my design vocabulary. I began asking questions I previously skipped: Who is harmed if this fails silently? What does “success” mean for a caregiver versus a clinician versus a developer? Those questions do not replace metrics; they sit alongside them. In a world where generative tools are one click away, that course helped me articulate why structure - schemas, retrieval, validation - can be more ethical than unconstrained fluency.

Advanced database management and quantitative analysis deserve mention because they grounded my ML work in stable data practice. It is difficult to build trustworthy models on ambiguous joins or inconsistent keys. Learning dimensional modeling and careful SQL transformations made my later projects faster to debug because the data layer was less mysterious.

Coursework that quietly supported everything else

Not every course produced a flashy GitHub repository, but several created habits that show up everywhere. Applied machine learning reinforced cross-validation discipline and honest comparisons between baselines and more complex models. Deep learning taught me to respect compute budgets and to interpret learning curves without romanticizing them - plateaus and overfitting stories are written in those curves if you look carefully. Natural language processing connected token-level mechanics to downstream behavior, which later mattered when I built retrieval pipelines and evaluated hallucination risk.

Business analytics reminded me that the stakeholder meeting starts with a question, not with a notebook. I practiced translating vague prompts into measurable targets, and I learned to anticipate the follow-up questions executives ask: cost, timeliness, and what changes if the model is wrong. Those soft-skill moments are easy to dismiss in favor of coding, but they determine whether a project ever leaves the sandbox.

The best parts - and the surprises

The best part of the program has been building a portfolio that looks like the work I want to do professionally: multimodal problems, generative AI used responsibly, and geospatial analytics with civic impact. The through-line is not any single algorithm; it is judgment - knowing what to build, what to validate, and how to explain uncertainty.

The biggest surprise was how social and contextual the field is. Data science is not solitary coding; it is clarifying questions, negotiating definitions of labels, and aligning metrics with values. Another surprise was how fast the tooling ecosystem moves - what matters is learning how to learn: reading papers, reading documentation, and building small prototypes that de-risk ideas quickly.

I was also surprised by how often the limiting factor was not model sophistication but data governance: access, documentation, and understanding lineage. In school projects you can sometimes simulate cleanliness; in internship settings you learn that pipelines are social agreements between teams. That realization made me a better partner in code reviews and a more patient debugger when something “should work” but does not - because the issue is often upstream.

What I would tell myself on day one

If I could send a note to my first-day self, I would say: spend more time on problem framing, keep a lab notebook mentality for experiments, and document decisions as you go - not as a chore, but as a way to think. I would also say: seek out internships and research early, not only for the resume line, but because they force you to reconcile classroom assumptions with real constraints. I would remind myself that the goal is not to collect every certificate, but to build a coherent story of how you think.

I would also emphasize community. Graduate school can feel competitive, but peers are the people who will debug your code at midnight, rehearse your presentation, and share job postings. I learned as much from whiteboard sessions and team projects as from lectures.

Closing thoughts

When I started, I wanted stronger technical skills. I leave with those skills - and with a clearer sense of responsibility: to scope problems honestly, to validate claims carefully, and to communicate in ways that empower people rather than impress them. The projects on this site are not perfect; they are stepping stones. Together, they represent the kind of practitioner I am becoming: someone who cares about the full pipeline, the end user, and the integrity of the story the data tells.

Looking ahead, I want roles where I can continue building at the intersection of rigorous modeling and human impact - whether that means safer interfaces for medical information, more reliable analytics for cities, or more careful evaluation of predictive systems in finance. The program gave me a toolkit; the next step is to keep earning trust with every release, every figure, and every honest explanation of what I do not yet know.

If you are reading this as a prospective student or hiring manager, I hope the takeaway is simple: this degree did not hand me a single recipe - it trained me to learn recipes under pressure, to document them clearly, and to choose responsibly when the stakes are high.
